The Panorama as an archive: Curating Black Memory in the Virtual Space

Mihlali Mabo

The virtual curation I designed is a curation that intersects critically with my research interests. My research is concerned with the ontological resonances between the San rock art of the Eastern Cape Drakensberg and contemporary Black painting in Southern Africa. It particularly focuses on bodies that refuse notions of closure and looks into Black figuration and abstraction as ontological necessity. The research thesis comparatively examines rock art panels that span over 30,000 years and contemporary abstract and figurative painters, such as Dumile Feni and Portia Zvavahera. Through this analysis, I argue that these art practices bear similar formal characteristics, a practice that embodies a sense of resistance. In both these practices, they alter the figures in ways that they appear fragmented, dissolved, multi-layered and anatomically distorted. These effects are not only stylistically familiar but are grounded in navigating similar ontological conditions. Theoretically, it grounds the research in Timothy Morton’s concept of hyperobjects, where he states that hyperobjects are entities distributed in time or space far beyond human scale and comprehension. In addition, it considers Umberto Eco’s theory of open works, which posits that artworks are structurally resistant to ideas of closure and rely on subsequent participation for ongoing completion. Essentially, my research aims to demonstrate that the colonial rupture attempted to temporally dislocate Black indigenous art practices. This evidence is displayed in the San rock art being relegated to a ‘primitive’ past while simultaneously continuing this practice in the contemporary with Black abstraction and figuration being framed as ‘emerging’ from European modernity. However, the thesis argues that this attempt was thwarted because the epistemological rupture could not sever the ontological entanglement.
